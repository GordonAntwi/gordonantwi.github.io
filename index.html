The Study of Human Behavior

By Antwi Gordon

As someone who has studied the core of human thought and action, I recognize that the drive to excel is one of the most powerful forces in human behavior. We strive, compete, and often base our entire self-worth on the idea of being the best—the best employee, the best parent, the most successful entrepreneur.

This endless cycle of comparison and striving is fundamentally exhausting, and it’s a behavior pattern that even science’s greatest mind, Albert Einstein, advised against.

Einstein, whose achievements define genius, wasn’t just a physicist; he was a profound observer of the human condition. He understood the psychological trap of defining oneself by external achievement, and he offered a powerful alternative metric for a meaningful life.

The Tyranny of Success

When people act from a place of needing to be "the best," their motivation often stems from external measures. It’s about the title, the salary, the accolades, or the social proof that others can see. This pursuit is what Einstein called the pursuit of Success, and he gave us a critical piece of advice to reframe our thinking: “Try not to become a man of success, but rather try to become a man of value.”

Success, in the popular sense, is a fleeting, comparative state. It requires a constant, anxious gaze outward to see who you are beating or who is ahead of you. This focus on external outcomes is why many people, despite achieving traditional success, still feel a deep sense of inadequacy or emptiness.

The Liberation of Value

The shift to **Value** completely transforms how you think and act. Value is about contribution. It’s internal, measurable only by your own sense of purpose and the positive impact you create—whether it’s for a single person, your team, or your community.

The moment you choose to create value, you are liberated from the comparison game. You stop asking, "Am I better than them?" and start asking, "Am I serving a purpose bigger than myself?" This shift aligns with Einstein’s view on ego itself: he described the feeling of being a separate individual—restricted to personal desires and affections—as an “optical delusion of consciousness.” By focusing on value, you widen your "circle of compassion" and concern beyond just yourself. This is the moment your behavior moves from ego-driven anxiety to purpose-driven action.

The Process of Changing Your Thinking

Our actions are always a reflection of our internal thinking. If you constantly feel like you’re falling short, it’s because your internal metric (your thinking) is based on competition. This need to be flawless is why most people never accept their mistakes—because admitting a mistake feels like admitting they are not 'the best,' often leading to feelings of deep disgrace.

This difficulty is compounded by the fact that one of the hardest things in life is simply speaking the truth, both to others and to ourselves. Furthermore, most people are perfectly capable of speaking, but they cannot structure their thoughts effectively or frame their ideas in a way that truly honors the listener, meaning their intended value often gets lost in poor presentation, which creates friction for the opposer and inevitably leads to communication breakdown or failure. Remember: if you have never been a fool in a situation you can never fully understand, even if someone is speaking something that is wise or seems perfectly structured. However, **embracing error is, in fact, the best way to move forward.**

As Einstein observed: "The world as we have created it is a process of our thinking. It cannot be changed without changing our thinking." To improve your behavior and self-view, you must change the fundamental thought process driving your actions. Stop using "best" as your benchmark. Start using "better contribution," "clearer purpose," and "deeper meaning."

When your purpose is to add value, your actions become meaningful regardless of external validation, and that is a far more reliable source of human fulfillment. This humility also extends to relationships: learn to say sorry, whether the person you wronged is a child, an adult, or anyone else. It's the ultimate expression of prioritizing connection over ego. This is the honest declaration that frees you from the ego's trap: "I'm not the best, but I am always learning."
